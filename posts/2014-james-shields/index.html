Ah, the 2014 Kansas City Royals. The team of destiny, after not reaching the postseason for 29 years, they became the little engine that could. But they almost didn’t even make it out of the first round of the playoffs. I remember as a 14-year-old kid sitting in my parents basement watching the AL Wild Card game. James Shields got the start for the Royals, going up against Jon Lester, who was acquired by the Athletics mid-season. James Shields proceeded to allow an early home run to Brandon Moss, being relieved by Yordano Ventura who allowed another home run to Brandon Moss. Three weeks later that Royals team lost to the San Francisco Giants in the World Series.

But the Royals had to make the World Series somehow, and James Shields was the workhorse of the Royals pitching staff. James Shields was traded to the Kansas City Royals from the Tampa Bay Rays along with bullpen ace Wade Davis, for Rookie of the Year winner Wil Meyers and Jake Odorizzi. The Royals knew they were close to contending and grabbing Shields was their signal to fans they cared about winning.

But Shields wasn’t the best pitcher in the MLB, and the Royals didn’t need him to be. He was consistent and was an innings eater. He didn’t allow a ton of runs either, being a guy who threw every 5 days, finishing with over 200 innings.

But how did Shields compare in ERA+ with the top 25 starters in 2014?

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(ggrepel)
library(scales)

pitchers <- read_csv("2014eraleaders.csv")

kershaw <- pitchers %>% filter(Name == "Clayton Kershaw*")
lester <- pitchers %>% filter(Name == "Jon Lester*")
shields <- pitchers %>% filter(Name == "James Shields")


ggplot() + 
  geom_bar(
    data=pitchers, 
    aes(
      x=reorder(Name, -`ERA+`), 
      weight=`ERA+`
      ), fill="light grey") +
  geom_bar(
    data=shields, 
    aes(
      x=reorder(Name, -`ERA+`), 
      weight=`ERA+`
      ), fill="#BD9B60"
    ) + 
  geom_bar(
    data=kershaw, 
    aes(
      x=reorder(Name, -`ERA+`), 
      weight=`ERA+`
      ), fill="#005A9C"
  ) +
 geom_bar(
    data=lester, 
    aes(
      x=reorder(Name, -`ERA+`), 
      weight=`ERA+`
      ), fill="#003831"
  ) +
  scale_y_continuous(labels=comma) +
  labs(
    x="Pitchers",
    y="ERA+",
    title="Leaders in ERA+ in the MLB in 2014",
    subtitle="James Shields was slightly above average, but he was no Kershaw",
    caption="Source: https://www.baseball-reference.com/leagues/majors/2014-standard-pitching.shtml | By: Jackson Reddick"
  ) +
  theme_minimal() + 
    theme(
    plot.title = element_text(size = 16, face = "bold"),

    plot.subtitle = element_text(size=10),
    panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
    axis.text.x = element_blank()

Looking at the ERA+ statistic is a pitcher’s ERA adjusted to their respective ballparks. Looking at Kershaw’s at the top is an absolute god like number at almost 200. League average is 100, so Shields sitting at 123 is a respective number for an ace pitcher. Lester sitting at 156 showed why he was such a tough matchup for the Royals offense in the AL Wild Card Game.

For me, looking at a pitchers WHIP statistic tells a big story about how good of a pitcher they are. Combining walks and hits allowed per inning into a statistic is a huge measuring stick to how good of a pitcher they are. So, let’s take a look at James Shields compared to the Top 25 pitchers in the MLB in 2014.

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(ggrepel)

mlbpitchers <- read_csv("2014royalssp.csv")

whip <- mlbpitchers %>% 
  group_by(Name) %>% 
  summarise(
    Innings = sum(IP), 
    TotalWHIP = sum(WHIP)
    ) %>% na.omit()

felix <- whip %>% filter(Name == "Felix Hernandez")
bumgarner <- whip %>% filter(Name == "Madison Bumgarner*")
shields <- whip %>% filter(Name == "James Shields")

ggplot() + 
  geom_point(
    data=whip, 
    aes(x=Innings, y=TotalWHIP), color="grey",
    alpha=.5) +
  geom_point(
    data=felix, aes(x=Innings, y=TotalWHIP), color="#005C5C"
  ) +
  geom_point(
    data=bumgarner,aes(x=Innings, y=TotalWHIP), color="#FD5A1E"
  ) +
  geom_point(
    data=shields, aes(x=Innings, y=TotalWHIP), color="#BD9B60"
  ) +
  scale_y_continuous(labels=comma) +
  geom_text(aes(x=227, y=.91, label="Felix Hernandez"), size=3, color="#005C5C") +
  geom_text(aes(x=207, y=1.09, label="Madison Bumgarner"), size=3, color="#FD5A1E") +
  geom_text(aes(x=235, y=1.18, label="James Shields"), size=3, color="#BD9B60") +
  labs(
    x="Innings Pitched",
    y="WHIP (BB + H) per IP",
    title="Total WHIP Leaders in 2014",
    subtitle="James Shields threw a ton of innings, and held a respectable WHIP",
    caption="Source: https://www.baseball-reference.com/leagues/majors/2014-standard-pitching.shtml | By: Jackson Reddick"
  ) +
  theme_minimal() + 
    theme(
    plot.title = element_text(size = 16, face = "bold"),
    axis.title = element_text(size = 8),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size=10),
    panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
)

James Shields threw a ton of innings, which means his WHIP will be a tad inflated, but he showed that he was a quality starting pitcher nonetheless. Felix Hernandez is forgotten so much in MLB history, and his dominance was never loved enough. And there is the devil himself Madison Bumgarner. Yeah we can end with that, go watch 2014 World Series highlights for more details.

Another huge stat for me is looking at pitcher’s strikeout to walk ratio, as strikeouts aren’t the most important, but walks are a killer. I wanted to compare Shields to an AL Central great pitcher in Corey Kluber, who when in Cleveland was a damn killer. Kluber was as dominant as they come, but what do the numbers say?

Code 
library(waffle)
library(ggrepel)

mlbpitchers <- read_csv("2014royalssp.csv")

shields <- c("SO"=180, 89, "BB"=44)
kluber <- c("SO"=269, "BB"=51)

iron(
 waffle(shields, 
        rows = 10, 
        title="James Shields SO/BB Ratio", 
        xlab="1 square = 1 SO/BB", 
        colors = c("#BD9B60", "white", "#004687")
        ),
 waffle(kluber, 
        rows = 10, 
        title="Corey Kluber SO/BB Ratio", 
        xlab="1 square = 1 SO/BB", 
        colors = c("#00385D", "#E50022")
        )
)

Kluber dominated Shields in the strikeout department, but Shields still held up with not walking a ton of guys either. One guy uses his dominance to strikeout hitters and one uses finesse to get hitters out. There are multiple ways to win a major league baseball game and both these guys show that.

In conclusion the 2014 Kansas City Royals needed James Shields in order to be successful. Was he the man that propelled them to the World Series? Probably not. But was he a key cog in the machine that got the Royals into the Fall Classic? Absolutely.
